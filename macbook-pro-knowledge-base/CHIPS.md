## Generation Architecture Notes

### M2 (Jan 2023, TSMC 5nm N5P)
The M2 generation was Apple's second-gen Apple Silicon in the MacBook Pro. The M2 Max packed 67 billion transistors with a 400 GB/s memory bus that remains competitive with M4 Pro chips two generations later. Core naming: P-cores (performance) + E-cores (efficiency). All M2 Pro/Max configs share the same die, with lower-bin chips having disabled cores.

### M3 (Nov 2023, TSMC 3nm N3B)
First 3nm chips in a Mac. Introduced hardware ray tracing and dynamic caching for GPU workloads. The M3 base chip was notable for being the first "base" chip in a 14-inch MacBook Pro, starting at just $1,599 with 8GB RAM (widely criticized as too little). The M3 Max 16-core/40-GPU pushed to 92 billion transistors. Memory bandwidth was notably lower on M3 Pro (150 GB/s) compared to M2 Pro (200 GB/s), which disappointed LLM users.

### M4 (Nov 2024, TSMC 3nm N3E)
The M4 generation brought a massive Neural Engine upgrade to 38 TOPS (from 18), Thunderbolt 5 on Pro/Max models, a 12MP camera, and 1,000 nit SDR brightness. The M4 Max 16-core/40-GPU achieved 546 GB/s bandwidth. Process node refinement (N3E vs N3B) improved power efficiency significantly. M4 Pro/Max were the first MacBook Pro chips with TB5 at 120 Gb/s.

### M5 (Oct 2025 base, Mar 2026 Pro/Max, TSMC 3nm N3P)
The M5 Pro and M5 Max introduced Apple's "Fusion Architecture," bonding two 3nm dies using TSMC SoIC-mH packaging. Core naming changed: "super cores" replaced P-cores, while a new "performance core" tier was added between super and efficiency cores. M5 Pro/Max brought WiFi 7 via the new Apple N1 wireless chip, Bluetooth 6, and SSD speeds up to 14.5 GB/s. The M5 Max 40-GPU achieves 614 GB/s bandwidth, the highest in any laptop.
